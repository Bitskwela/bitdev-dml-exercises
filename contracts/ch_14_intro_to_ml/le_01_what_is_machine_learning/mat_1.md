## The Concept: ML in Plain Language

### Definition

**Machine Learning (ML)** is a way of programming where, instead of typing every rule, you show the computer many examples and let it discover the rules itself.

### Rules vs. Learning

| Traditional programming | Machine learning |
|---|---|
| You write the rules | The computer finds the rules |
| `if payday and rainy: ulam = "sinigang"` | "Here are 977 days of sales. Find the pattern." |
| Breaks on cases you forgot | Smooths over cases you didn't think of |
| Scales with your patience | Scales with how much data you collect |

### The Three Steps Every ML Project Has

1. **Get data.** Tita Malou's notebook, an export, a dataset. Without data, no ML.
2. **Train a model.** Pick a math structure and let it adjust to fit your data.
3. **Use the model.** Feed in new inputs; get back a prediction.

That's it. The rest is detail — and we'll spend 24 more lessons on it.

### Filipino ML You Already Use

| Service | What the model learns |
|---|---|
| **Lazada search** | Which products match a vague Taglish query — from billions of past clicks |
| **Maya fraud detection** | Which transactions look suspicious — from years of flagged-vs-clean data |
| **Waze ETA** | How long a route will take — from GPS pings of every Waze driver |

Nobody at Lazada typed a rule that says *"if query contains 'pang' weight formalwear."* The system *learned* it.

---

## Key Takeaways

- **ML replaces hand-written rules with learned patterns.** You stop typing rules; you start collecting examples.
- **Every ML project has three steps:** get data, train a model, use the model.
- **Tita Malou is already a model** — trained on 15 years of carinderia data.
- **Examples scale better than rules.** Adding a row is easier than adding a rule.
- **The shift is conceptual, not mathematical** — before any algorithm, you have to believe data can replace logic.
